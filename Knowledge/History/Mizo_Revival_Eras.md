# Mizo Revival Eras: The Spiritual Transformation

The "Revivals" (*Harhna*) are a series of pivotal spiritual movements that fundamentally reshaped Mizo society, transforming it from a traditional tribal structure into one of the most devout Christian societies in Asia.

## 🕊️ Theoretical Framework
Mizo revivals are characterized by "ecstatic collective experience," often involving congregational singing, dancing (*Lama*), and communal weeping. They acted as a psychological bridge during the rapid transition from the old world to the new.

## 🗓️ The Four Major Eras

### 1. The First Revival (1906)
- **Origin**: Started at the Mizo church in Aizawl after attendees returned from a Welsh Mission meeting in Khasi Hills (Mairang).
- **Effect**: It was the first "spark" that broke traditional village skepticism.

### 2. The Second Revival (1913 - 1914)
- **Characteristics**: Known for its "moral reform." Thousands of people publicly confessed crimes and social transgressions.
- **Societal Impact**: This era saw the large-scale abandonment of individual animal sacrifices and traditional spirit-appeasement.

### 3. The Third Revival (1919)
- **The "Khuang" Era**: It was during this revival that traditional drums (*Khuang*) were first brought into the church.
- **Cultural Synthesis**: This was a critical moment where Mizo musical heritage was "baptized" into the new faith, leading to the creation of the unique *Puitu* style of hymn singing.

### 4. The Fourth Revival (1930s)
- **Expansion**: This era solidified the church as the primary social institution of the Mizo people, completely replacing the *Zawlbuk* (bachelor's dormitory) as the center of community life.
