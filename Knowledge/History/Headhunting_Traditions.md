# Pre-Colonial Headhunting Traditions

**Tags:** #history #culture #warfare

Before the arrival of British missionaries and colonial authorities, headhunting was a significant part of the socio-religious life of some Mizo clans.

## Purpose of Headhunting
It was not merely an act of war. Headhunting was driven by several factors:
1. **Spiritual Merit:** Capturing the head of an enemy was believed to provide the warrior with a servant in the afterlife (**[[Mitthi_Khua]]**).
2. **Social Status:** A man could not wear certain prestigious clothing or ornaments without having taken a head. It was a requirement for the title of **[[Thangchhuah]]**.
3. **Territorial Defense:** It served as a powerful deterrent against rival clans.

## The Ritual
The captured heads were brought back to the village with great ceremony and placed on the **Lungpui** (stone platforms) or hung in the [[Zawlbuk]].

## Abolition
The practice was rapidly abandoned in the 1890s due to the combined influence of British military suppression and the spread of [[Christianity_in_Mizoram|Christianity]], which offered a new path to spiritual merit and social status.
