# The 1966 Aizawl Airstrike (Operation Red Horn)



The **1966 Aizawl Airstrike** remains the only time the Indian Air Force has used fighter jets against its own civilian population within its domestic territory. It was the climax of the **[[MNF_Uprising_1966]]**.

## Context: Operation Jericho
Following the declaration of independence by the **[[Mizo_National_Front]]** on February 28, 1966, MNF volunteers seized control of Aizawl, Lunglei, and other major towns. The Indian government, taken by surprise, launched **Operation Jericho** to retake the hills.

## The Bombing (March 5-6, 1966)
When the advancing Indian Army columns were blocked by MNF sharpshooters, the government deployed Toofani and Hunter aircraft from Tezpur. On March 5th and 6th, these jets bombarded parts of Aizawl, specifically the areas around the Treasury and the MNF headquarters. The resulting fires destroyed a significant portion of the town's thatched-roof houses.

## Human and Social Impact
The airstrike caused widespread panic and led to the massive displacement of civilians into the surrounding jungles. It serves as a traumatic memory in **[[Mizoram_Political_History]]** and was a primary driver for the subsequent **[[Village_Grouping]]** policy. The trauma of the "fire from the sky" became a central theme in the **[[Songs_of_the_Bamboo_Famine]]** and subsequent resistance literature.

## Legacy
While the airstrike was successful in "breaking" the MNF's urban control, it radicalized the population, leading to a 20-year-long insurgency. Peace was only restored with the **[[Mizo_Peace_Accord_1986]]**. Today, the event is discussed as a pivotal moment of national trauma and state-citizen friction.
