# Traditional Mizo Sculpture and Stone Carving



Traditional Mizo sculpture is primarily found in the form of megalithic stone carvings and memorial stones (**[[Mizo_Death_Rites_Thane]]**). Unlike the intricate wood carvings of some neighboring tribes, Mizo sculpture was often minimalist and symbolic, etched onto massive stone slabs.

## Memorial Stones (Lung)
The most common form of Mizo stone art involved commemorative pillars raised for great warriors and chiefs. These stones often featured carvings of:
- **Heads of animals**: Representing successful hunts (Mithun, stag, bear).
- **Human figures**: Representing the deceased and their attendants.
- **Spears and Weapons**: Denoting the status of the brave (*Pasaltha*).

## Famous Sites
Several sites across Mizoram preserve these ancient sculptures:
- **Kawtchhuah Ropui**: A heritage site in Champhai district featuring over 100 carved stone slabs.
- **Lungphunlian**: Known for its massive vertical monoliths with ancient engravings.

## Techniques and Significance
The stones were typically carved using iron chisels. The act of raising a stone was a major social event involving community labor and feasts, reinforcing the bonds of **[[Tlawmngaihna]]**. Today, these sculptures provide vital clues to **[[Mizo_Migration]]** patterns and pre-colonial beliefs.
