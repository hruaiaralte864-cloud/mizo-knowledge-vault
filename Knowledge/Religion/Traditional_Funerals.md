# Traditional Mizo Funerals: From Bones to Brotherhood

The Mizo approach to death is a profound expression of community solidarity. This node documents the transition from early tribal "deathways" to the modern, YMA-led funerary system.

## 🏺 Pre-Christian Funeral Rites
Before 1894, Mizo death rituals were centered on the continuity of the spirit.
- **Burial**: The dead were often buried under the floor of the house or very close to it.
- **Khuangchawi**: Wealthy families would perform complex rituals to ensure the deceased reached *Pialral* (Paradise) rather than *Mithi Khua* (The Village of the Dead).
- **Relic Preservation**: High-status chiefs sometimes had their remains periodically exhumed and cleaned—a practice known as "bone preservation" to maintain a physical bond with the ancestors.

## 🤝 Modern Funeral Culture & the YMA
Today, Mizo funerals are universally recognized as the highest example of **Mizo Tlawmngaihna** (Altruism).
- **The Role of YMA**: When a death occurs, the **Young Mizo Association (YMA)** immediately takes over. They dig the grave (*Thlan la*), manage the logistics, and prepare meals.
- **Mithi Ral**: Neighbors and family friends participate in an overnight wake (*Vakvari*), ensuring the grieving family is never left alone in the first 24-48 hours.
- **Khuang vaw**: Traditional drums are often beaten in a specific rhythm during the procession to signal the passing of a community member.

## 🪦 Symbolism & Cemeteries
Unlike the past, almost every Mizo village now has a hilltop cemetery (*Thlanmual*). Gravestones often include poetic epitaphs from the *Hlakungpui* (Poets) and specify the person's contribution to the church and society.
