# Khuang Rhythms and Traditional Notation



The **[[Khuang]]** (Mizo drum) is the heartbeat of Mizo communal life. Understanding its rhythmic patterns is essential to grasping the structure of Mizo folk dances and church hymns.

## The Three Primary Beats
While Mizo music is often transcribed in Western time signatures (usually 4/4 or 6/8), traditional Khuang playing follows three distinct rhythmic "feels":
1. **Lianpui Beat**: A deep, resonant slow beat used in somber ceremonies and communal mourning.
2. **Khuangpuitu Beat**: A syncopated, mid-tempo beat used to accompany the **[[Chheih_Lam]]** dance. It involves a rapid "double-tap" followed by a heavy central strike.
3. **Thangchhuah Beat**: A celebratory, fast-paced rhythm used during the high festivals (*Kut*).

## Notation and Transmission
Traditionally, Khuang rhythms were not written down but passed from master to apprentice through "mouth-notation" (onomatopoeic sounds like *Tum-Chak-Tum*). In recent years, scholars from the **[[Mizoram_University_MZU]]** music department have developed a standardized staff notation to preserve these polyrhythms.

## Social Role
The "Khuang-pup" (lead drummer) is a respected social position. The drummer must anticipate the emotional shifts of the singer and the energy of the crowd, embodying the spirit of **[[Tlawmngaihna]]** by providing the foundation for others to shine.
