# The Mizo Lens: A History of Photography in the Hills

**Tags:** #culture #media #history

The history of photography in Mizoram is a fascinating journey from "Imperial Surveillance" to "Cultural Self-Expression."

## Early Phase: Colonial Records (1890s-1940s)
The first cameras in the hills belonged to British officers and missionaries. These early photographs, now housed in the **[[Mizoram_Archives_Indo-Burma_Files]]**, provide a stark and often clinical view of Mizo life. They were used to document the **[[Lushai_Expeditions]]** and the progress of the **[[The_Great_Revival_1906]]**.

## Mid-Century: The Rise of Studio Culture
By the 1950s, Mizo photographers like **[[C_Lalhmingliana_Gallery]]** began setting up studios in Aizawl. This was the era of the "Puan Portrait," where families would dress in their finest **[[Mizo_Handicrafts_Textiles]]** to capture their image. These studios became vital social spaces during the **[[MNF_Uprising_1966]]**, as people used photos to stay connected with relatives in grouping centers.

## Modern Era: Digital Storytelling
Today, Mizo photographers are winning national awards for their "Humanist" approach to capturing the hills. The transition from film to digital has allowed for the rapid growth of **[[Mizo_Media_History]]**. Modern exhibitions focus on the "Unseen Mizoram," documenting the remote life in the **[[Mamit_District]]** and the changing face of the **[[Aizawl_District]]**.
