# Jhum Cultivation — Shifting Agriculture

**Tags:** #culture #economy #environment

**Jhum**, or slash-and-burn shifting cultivation, was the exclusive agricultural system of the Mizo people for centuries. The entire rhythm of traditional Mizo life — its festivals, migrations, and village layouts — was dictated by the Jhum cycle.

## The Annual Cycle
1. **Chap (January):** The Chief selects and marks a patch of bamboo or secondary jungle.
2. **Vat (January–February):** Men fell all the trees with large *dao* (machetes).
3. **Chapchar wait (March):** Felled material dries. [[Chapchar_Kut]] is celebrated during this pause.
4. **Hal (March–April):** The dried swath is set on fire. The ash powerfully fertilises the soil.
5. **Sawr (May–June):** With the monsoon rains, seeds of rice, maize, and vegetables are sow simultaneously using pointed dibble sticks.
6. **Thlai seng (October–December):** Crops are harvested; [[Pawl_Kut]] is celebrated.

## Ecological Crisis
Because soil fertility depletes rapidly, fields were traditionally abandoned for 10–15 years to regenerate. Modern population growth has compressed this cycle to 2–4 years, causing severe forest degradation and soil erosion. The government's **New Land Use Policy (NLUP)** attempts to transition farmers from Jhum to settled horticulture.
