# Aeronautical Metaphors in Mizo Song and Poetry



A unique characteristic of modern Mizo songwriting, especially in the 1960s-80s, is the recurring use of "aeronautical metaphors" (Airplanes, Flight, Pilots) to describe social and spiritual transition.

## The Airplane as a Symbol of Freedom
During the **[[Mizo_Insurgency_Movement]]**, the sound of aircraft was often associated with conflict. However, songwriters like **[[Rokunga]]** and others transformed this into a metaphor for the soul's flight toward peace or heaven.
- **Example**: The "Silver Bird" (*Chawng-zawl-lei*) was a common term for aircraft, used to describe the bridge between the isolated hills and the vast outside world.

## The Pilot as Leader
The "Pilot" often served as a metaphor for a visionary leader or a spiritual guide navigating the difficult "clouds" of Mizo history. This reflects the Mizo ability to integrate modern technology into their ancient **[[Mizo_Oral_Tradition]]**.

## Impact on Pop Music
Even today, in the **[[Mizo_Music_Digital_Distribution]]** era, lyrics often reference "taking flight" or "climbing the skies," rooted in this 20th-century lyrical trend. It illustrates the tribe's aspiration for progress and global connectivity (**[[Governance_Overview]]**).
