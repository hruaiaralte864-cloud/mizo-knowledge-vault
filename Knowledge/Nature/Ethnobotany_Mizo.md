# Mizo Ethnobotany — Plants in Culture

**Tags:** #nature #culture #medicine

The Mizo people accumulated centuries of empirical knowledge about the medicinal and practical properties of their forest plants — a body of knowledge largely held by elder women and former Bawlpu (shamans).

## Medicinal Plants

**Kawlbuk (Bamboo shoots)**
Young bamboo shoots are consumed extensively in fermented and fresh forms. Traditionally believed to aid digestion and reduce fever.

**Fak (Wild Turmeric — Curcuma)**
Applied topically on wounds and skin infections. Consumed in soups during illness.

**Theizawng (Wild Ginger)**
Extensively used for stomach complaints, nausea, and cold-related symptoms. Made into a burning hot infusion.

**Riloh (Neem relatives)**
Bark and leaves boiled into bitter tonics for malaria management.

**Tlangsam (Eupatorium)**
A common hedge plant used as an antiseptic poultice on wounds and insect bites.

## Loss of Knowledge
With the transition from traditional shamanism (*Bawlpu*) to Christian medicine and modern healthcare, much of this oral pharmacopoeia is not being transmitted to the next generation, creating an urgent ethnobotanical documentation crisis.
